1. Project Overview

The Ramayana RAG Project was created to transform the sacred text of the Valmiki Ramayana into a queryable, high-fidelity knowledge archive. The goal was to build a system that moves beyond simple keyword matching to perform "Deep Reasoning" over narrative causality, character motivations, and chronological events.

2. Problem Statement & Research Journey

The primary objective of this research was to investigate a fundamental question in Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems: Which embedding models, retrieval strategies, and chunking techniques perform best for different knowledge domains?

Our team initially identified and categorized 10 different RAG problem domains, each representing a unique type of knowledge retrieval challenge. These domains included areas such as technical documentation, research papers, conversational data, and mythological literature.

Chosen Domain: Mythological Knowledge Retrieval

From the identified categories, I selected the mythological domain and decided to build a RAG system focused on the Valmiki Ramayana.

Mythological texts present unique challenges for RAG systems:

  • Complex narrative structures
  • Multiple names for the same character
  • Chronological storytelling across large texts
  • Heavy use of symbolic and poetic language

Dataset Exploration

The first challenge was to identify a reliable dataset suitable for building the RAG system. I explored multiple sources and experimented with different text formats.

1. Sanskrit Source

Initially, I attempted to use the Sanskrit version of the Ramayana from:

https://sanskritdocuments.org/mirrors/ramayana/valmiki.htm

However, working directly with the Sanskrit corpus proved extremely difficult due to linguistic complexity and inconsistent formatting for automated chunking.

2. Sanskrit-English (Sansglish)

The next attempt involved a hybrid Sanskrit-English text (Sansglish).

Despite experimentation, the semantic retrieval quality remained inconsistent and produced low-relevance context for complex narrative queries.

3. Final Dataset Selection

After extensive exploration, a clean English translation was selected:

https://www.ramayana.lauragibbs.net/ramayana.pdf

This version provided structured narrative text that allowed effective preprocessing, chunking, and semantic embedding generation, enabling high-accuracy narrative reasoning.

2. Strategy: Story-Aware Process

Unlike traditional RAG systems that use arbitrary character or token-based chunking, this project employed a "Story-Aware" strategy to preserve narrative integrity.

Narrative Chunking

Self-Contained Units

The text was parsed into 202 distinct story chunks, each representing a complete event (e.g., "Ravana's Birth").

Structural Parsing

Regex-based parsers detected story boundaries, ensuring no narrative was cut mid-sentence.

LLM Verification: Every chunk was validated by an LLM (Groq/Ollama) to ensure semantic completeness and accuracy before indexing.

11. Scholar-Voice Playground

Example of high-fidelity reasoning over mythological context.

Why was Ravana unable to be killed by gods or demons?
According to the Bala Kanda, Ravana performed severe penance for ten thousand years to please Lord Brahma. Tactical Insight: He specifically requested immunity from Devas, Danavas, Gandharvas, and Rakshasas. In his arrogance, he did not ask for protection from humans or monkeys, considering them insignificant. Conclusion: This boon created a strategic vulnerability that Lord Vishnu fulfilled by incarnating as Rama, a human.

12. Hallucination Monitoring & Quality Gates

To ensure 100% adherence to the Valmiki tradition, the system employs automated quality gates.

Context Adherence (Faithfulness)

Measures how much of the response is grounded strictly in the retrieved story chunks. Threshold: > 0.9

Answer Relevance

Ensures the response directly addresses the user's query without tangential details. Threshold: > 0.85

Automated Safety Gates

Metric Failure Action Safety Logic
Low Faithfulness Regenerate with High Temp Contextual fallback to source text only
Entity Mismatch Flag for Human Review Prevents mixing characters across different Kandas
Timeline Breach Rerank Chunks Ensures event A is cited before event B

13. Domain Adaptation: Generic RAG Strategy

Validated whether the same RAG architecture could generalize across other types of knowledge domains.

Critical Finding: While the core pipeline (BAAI/bge-m3 + LanceDB) was technically adapted, the "Story-Aware" strategy was not suitable for other domains. Direct porting resulted in poor performance, proving that each knowledge system requires its own bespoke retrieval and chunking strategy.

SOP Knowledge RAG

Attempted transition; required Policy-based chunking rather than Narrative episodes.

huggingface.co/spaces/Vanshcc/SOP_RAG
Constitution of India

Attempted transition; required Article-specific indexing rather than Story-ordering.

huggingface.co/spaces/Vanshcc/IC
Git Doc Rerank

Attempted transition; required functional command grouping to be effective.

huggingface.co/spaces/Vanshcc/GIT_DOC_RAG

Final Research Conclusion

The experiments confirmed that a one-size-fits-all approach is insufficient for production RAG. While the Ramayana strategy achieved 100% accuracy for mythological narratives, adapting it to legal or technical contexts required fundamental changes to the data processing layers to maintain relevance.

14. System Architecture & End-to-End Workflow

An exhaustive look at the internal data movement and processing pipeline.

Frontend & Inference

  • Framework: Streamlit 1.35+
  • Primary API: Groq (Llama-3.3-70B)
  • Resilient Fallback: SambaNova (Llama-3.1-70B)
